Software Project Engineering Management - Learning Outcomes

  • Identify and apply appropriate software engineering and project management methodologies, tools and techniques for the development of solutions to real-world problems.

  • Explore the implications of computer and network architectures for system-level design and development, as appropriate for risk and quality management.

  • Design, develop and evaluate management systems to produce solutions that handle constraints and uncertainty, contextualising BDD concepts and secure coding principles & practices in SDLC.

  • Systematically develop and implement the skills required to be effective member of a development team in a virtual professional environment, adopting real-life perspectives on roles and team organisation.

    • Introduction to Software Engineering Project Management

    Learning Outcomes

    • Describe a number of large projects, and the reasons for failure.
    • Explain the relationship between Project Management, TOGAF and the SDLC.
    • Describe the differences between the development of the Software Engineering and Project Management disciplines.

    Reflection

    In the first week of the module, all participants got assigned to project groups; Group 1 and Group 2. In the past, I have had both positive and unexpected negative experiences whilst working in a group but this time, being the third time, i feel better prepared. I have some course mates in my group whom i had previously worked with in other modules so I am familiar with their style of work as well as their strengths. To facilitate communication amongst ourselves, I set up a discord channel and added the rest of the team. I also sent off our team contract to the module tutor

    Our first collaborative discussion on “Project Failures” was an insightful one. We discussed some notable past troublesome projects and learned the reasons why they failed. To summarise, every project failure can be categorised under four major areas; Tasks, people, methods and environment according to Lehtinen et al. (2014). I believe having this discussion early on in the module is a good way to create awareness amongst course participants and prepare us for the summative group assessments ahead. It also gives an appreciation of what could go wrong in a real-life project team. Ultimately, I am more aware of contributory factors to the success or failure of a project.

    Reference

    Lehtinen, A., Mäntylä, V., Vanhanen, J., Itkonen, J. & Lassenius, C. (2014) Perceived causes of software project failures – An analysis of their relationships. Information and Software Technology 56(6): 623–643.

    Software Development Life Cycles

    Learning Outcomes

    • Describe the activities that are carried out by a Project Manager at each stage of the SDLC.
    • Collect the requirements of a software engineering project using the Volere template.
    • Define a behaviour-driven development scenario.

    Reflection

    In week 3, my group (Team 1) set up a meeting with group 2 to discuss project requirements. Both teams played the role of Customer and developer and asked questions to clarify requirements on each party’s list. The tutor was also present to observe and provide guidance where necessary. We had a productive session and achieved all that we set out to do. The outcome of the meeting showed the importance of good planning and preparation in project management. My group had requested the requirements prior to the meeting and already had some key questions prepared, making the discussion between both parties a smooth sailing one.

    The lecturecast titled Software Development life cycle explained phases of software development life cycle and activities conducted by developers at each stage. The section of the lecturecast that covered mistakes made in software engineering is invaluable particularly because we had been discussing the causes of project failure and it was interesting to discover some of the mistakes mentioned by Ghazi et al (1999) (such as unsystematic or insufficient testing, requirement and quality features not determined, unrealistic deadlines) are still happening today. A typical example is the Ariane 5 disaster which failed due to requirements and testing shortfalls. The lecturecast material and our collaborative discussion has broadened my knowledge on causes of project failures. If I get the opportunity to work as part of any project management team in future, I am confident that I can make valuable contributions on how to avoid project pitfalls.

    For the formative task, we were required to read the learning material Dicheva & Hodge (2018) and then analyse the data structures of a frequently used online system. This activity opened up opportunities for research and critical thinking as I had never given a thought to data structures implemented by systems I use regularly.

    Python Data Structures

    Learning Outcomes

    • Identify the most appropriate data structure to retain different attributes of data.
    • Use Python’s built-in capabilities to practically retain data electronically.

    Reflection

    The second seminar of the module took place this week. For the seminar preparation exercise, I read about a modern product on the IEEE Spectrum website, Prosthetic hand which was designed to provide a sense of touch to the user.

    I practised using the Volere requirements template to capture a few requirements of the Prosthetic hands. The Volere template helps organisations save a lot of time in requirements gathering and also ensures that requirements are appropriately and adequately captured by including details on requirement rationale, originator, fit criterion, dependencies and supporting documentation (Volere, n.d). I found this task very insightful as I was able to learn important sections to include when capturing user requirements.

    For our group assignment, we had a follow up meeting with Group 2 to agree on the requirements to be accepted and those to be rejected. Requirements gathering is known to be one of the most difficult phases of project management as identifying customer’s true needs can be difficult (Larson & Larson, 2015). I did not get this experience during our meetings with Group 2 as there were no arguments on what requirements to be accepted or detailed discussion on customer’s most desired features in a product.

    I think having teams play a dual role of customer and developer created a situation where one team played nice to the other team so the same gesture could be reciprocated. This factor, in my opinion, did not closely portray a real life project management situation or attract genuine participation from students.

    Reference

    Estimating, Planning and Risk

    Learning Outcomes

    • Select and utilise an appropriate tool for planning.
    • Select and utilise an appropriate tool for estimating.
    • Select and utilise an appropriate tool for risk assessment.

    Reflection

    Our group had a strategy meeting on how to approach the week 11 assignment. My teammates appear to be more motivated and enthusiastic probably because of the low grades we achieved in the first summative assessment and the need to do better in the second one . I observed increased participation in our discussions and in depth research compared to the first assignment and as such I am looking forward to a better group performance this time.

    We split the teams into two smaller groups; one group focusing on code development and the other group, developing the presentation file. I consider presentation skills one of my great strengths so I decided to join the presentation team but I asked to also be involved in testing. Our group will most likely be using Github Actions to automate the required tests and I am quite keen on learning more about the software development features of github.

    This week’s Lecturecast titled “Estimating, Planning and Risk” taught various Risk Management frameworks, estimation methods and planning tools. After studying the various estimation methods, we decided to go with the expert judgement method because it is based on use cases and can be done earlier in the SDLC to give developers good room for adequate planning (Lecturecast, 2021). However, more accurate results have been achieved using a hybrid approach so the tutor advised combining expert judgement with planning poker for our summative assessment. I agree with incorporating planning poker as this estimation method allows all team members to be involved in the project estimation and be aware of resources and time involved in completing the project.

    Reference
  • Lecturecast (2021) Estimating, Planning and Risk. SPEM_PCOM7E. Software Project Engineering Management. University of Essex Online.

    Risk Assessment and Estimating Tools

    Learning Outcomes

    • Select an appropriate tool for project estimation based on a number of common factors.
    • Utilise the tool to create a project estimate.
    • Evaluate the usefulness of the tool in estimating.
    • Discuss risk evaluation and mitigation approaches.

    Reflection

    As part of the seminar 4 preparation exercise, I wrote a python code for the Expert Judgement estimation method and it worked really well. This task has helped to improve my coding skills and also my familiarity with the jupyter notebook tool. As an aspiring data scientist, I consider this a very valuable assignment because Jupyter notebook is one of the many tools used by Data Scientists on a daily basis.

    This week, I tried conducting some research to learn practical ways of applying any of the risk management frameworks taught in the last lecturecast (OCTAVE, openFAIR and NIST) but I had difficulties finding tools to achieve this. We had to use a common risk assessment matrix for our risk analysis.

    We also decided to go with the Program Evaluation Review Technique (P.E.R.T) estimation method instead of Expert Judgement as the former seemed better suited for our work. Rather than using a fixed time for each task, the Program Evaluation & Review Technique uses three-time estimates, taking into consideration some measure of uncertainty that might be associated with the actual completion of a task (Douglas, 1978). PERT will be used in combination with planning poker.
